Fall is our busiest season at Portland Picture Company, and it's not hard to understand why. The light goes golden, the foliage turns, and the Pacific Northwest looks like it was designed for portrait photography.

What you wear to your fall session matters more than most people expect. Here's what we recommend.

## Build Around a Color Palette, Not Matching Outfits

The most common mistake families make: everyone wears the same thing. Matching outfits tend to look dated and can flatten the visual interest of a group shot.

Instead, choose a palette of three or four colors and let each person pick what works for them within that range. For fall, we love:

- **Warm neutrals**: camel, cream, oatmeal, warm white
- **Earthy tones**: rust, terra cotta, burnt orange, olive
- **Muted accents**: sage green, dusty blue, burgundy

Everyone in the same palette reads as coordinated without looking like a uniform.

## Lean Into Texture and Layers

Fall sessions look best when there's visual variety in the clothing. A chunky knit sweater next to a denim jacket next to a flowy dress creates depth in the image. Layering also means you can adjust throughout the session — a jacket on, jacket off gives you different looks without changing.

Fabrics to lean into: wool, denim, flannel, corduroy, knit, leather.
Fabrics to avoid: shiny synthetics, anything heavily wrinkled.

## Kids First, Then Coordinate Around Them

If you have small children, start with what they'll actually wear comfortably. Then build the adult outfits around whatever they're in. Trying to force a toddler into something uncomfortable is a guaranteed way to lose the session.

## What Doesn't Work on Camera

- **Busy patterns**: small checks, fine stripes, and dense prints create a visual vibration on camera called moiré
- **Large logos or graphic tees**: pull the eye to text instead of faces
- **Neon or very saturated colors**: they compete with fall foliage rather than complementing it
- **All black**: can work but tends to feel heavy in an outdoor fall setting

## Bring a Change of Clothes

If you have the time in your session, a second look gives you variety in the gallery. Often the second look ends up being a favorite. Something casual for the first half, something slightly dressier for the second — or vice versa.
