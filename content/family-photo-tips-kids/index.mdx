We'll be honest: the "everyone look at the camera and smile" approach rarely produces the best family photos. Kids especially tend to deliver exactly the expression you don't want when you ask them to perform.

After years of family sessions across Portland and the Pacific Northwest, here's what Portland Picture Company has learned actually works.

## Timing Is the Most Important Decision You'll Make

A well-rested, recently-fed child is a completely different subject from a tired, hungry one. Schedule your session for the time of day when your kids are typically at their best — often mid-morning for younger children. Avoid nap windows and avoid scheduling too close to meal times.

If possible, build in a snack break at the thirty-minute mark.

## Arrive and Play First

Don't try to start shooting the moment you arrive. Give everyone ten minutes to explore the location, burn off energy, and get comfortable. We use this time to observe how the family interacts naturally — which kids are performers, which are shy, who makes who laugh.

That information shapes everything we do for the rest of the session.

## We Direct the Activity, Not the Expression

Rather than asking for smiles, we create situations. "Chase each other to that tree." "Parents carry the kids." "Everyone find a rock." The resulting interactions produce real expressions — laughter, surprise, genuine affection — that manufactured poses never can.

## Keep It Moving

Fifteen minutes at one spot, then we move. Changing the environment keeps kids interested and engaged. It also gives you a more varied gallery.

## The Chaos Frames Are Often the Best Ones

The blurry kid running toward camera. The toddler having a moment. The dog breaking out of formation. Don't stress about these — often they're the frames that end up printed. Real families look real.

## Trust the Edit

You'll see a lot of frames in your gallery where someone's eyes are closed or someone's looking the wrong direction. We cull these carefully before delivery. What you receive are the genuine moments — and in our experience, families are always surprised by how many there are.
